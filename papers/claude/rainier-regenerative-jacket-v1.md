The Rainier Regenerative Jacket
A tenant-led circular infrastructure proposal for frontier AI compute
Civilization is the art of staying where the water is.  — a working principle from every civilization that outlasted its neighbors

1. The archaeological frame
Between roughly 14,800 and 5,500 years ago, the Sahara was green. Lakes, rivers, grasslands, elephants, cattle-herding cultures. Multiple independent lines of evidence — pollen, sediment, rock art, faunal remains — confirm this. The landscape transition that produced today's desert was primarily orbital, not human, but the response to it was civilizational. Cultures that mastered water and nutrient cycling (Nile, Tigris-Euphrates, Indus) ran for 3,000–5,000 years at continuous sites. Cultures that extracted without replenishment ran for 300–500 years and became archaeology.
The American Dust Bowl is the same pattern at a compressed timescale: productive grassland converted to dust in roughly thirty years of extractive farming. The Ogallala Aquifer beneath those same plains is now being drawn down at ten times its recharge rate.
Hyperscale AI compute is the newest, largest version of this pattern. The test is whether the labs building systems to help humanity reason across long time horizons can apply that same reasoning to their own infrastructure. That question has a right answer.
2. What happens to Rainier's water
Project Rainier at full 2.4 GW buildout will draw roughly 800 million gallons per year from the local watershed. Of that, 75–85% evaporates into the atmosphere through cooling tower operation and leaves the local system permanently. The remaining 15–25% exits as contaminated blowdown. Essentially none returns to the Kankakee or St. Joseph aquifer in usable form.
New Carlisle, Indiana sits in the Great Lakes Compact region with strict diversion rules, in an agricultural belt that depends on aquifer integrity, adjacent to communities already watching xAI's paused Memphis water project with attention. The political exposure is not hypothetical.
3. The frontier lab incoherence
Anthropic has publicly committed to covering 100% of grid upgrades for its data centers, investing in curtailment systems, and sharing AI's benefits with local communities. The February 2026 policy is explicit. At the same time, Anthropic's tenant share of Project Rainier will participate in one of the largest open-loop water extractions in the American Midwest, with no public reporting on tenant-level circular-economy metrics.
This is a consistency gap, not a values gap. Anthropic does not operate the facilities and therefore does not naturally own the metrics. The first frontier lab to negotiate tenant-level circular-economy reporting with its cloud partners — and to co-finance the regenerative infrastructure required to make those metrics meaningful — establishes the template every subsequent lab will be measured against.
4. The Regenerative Jacket at Rainier Phase 1
A bolt-on regenerative infrastructure facility operated as a separate Community Benefit Infrastructure entity, with Anthropic as named tenant partner. Four components, all using verified commercially available technology:
	•	HVAC condensate harvest: captures 50–150M gallons/year of distilled-grade water currently lost to evaporation; direct makeup for cooling tower operations.
	•	Waste-heat greenhouse: 40–60 acres of food production using 50 MW of recovered thermal energy, operated by established Midwest ag partner (Ceres Greenhouse Solutions design, local ag co-op operator).
	•	Closed-loop water treatment: ULPA filtration, pH-controlled circulation, polished makeup water suitable for closed coolant loops; zero discharge to municipal sewer during normal operation.
	•	Tenant-level reporting framework: public annual reporting of Anthropic's share of Rainier on PUE, WUE, and circular-economy metrics, negotiated with AWS.

The regenerative path compounds in three dimensions: financial (risk-mitigation value grows with company scale — at $800B valuation, a permit delay costs far more than at $350B), political (community license to operate deepens over time), and civilizational (the template effect: if this works at Rainier, it becomes the default for the next 50+ hyperscale AI sites). The extractive path does not compound. It depreciates.
7. Financing structure
Gross CapEx: $33–58M for Phase 1. Structured as Community Benefit Infrastructure entity to access:
	•	USDA REAP (Rural Energy for America Program): 25–50% of eligible ag infrastructure
	•	IRA clean-energy investment tax credit: 30% of eligible equipment
	•	Indiana state agricultural innovation grants: variable, 10–20%
	•	Opportunity Zone designation (site-dependent): capital gains deferral for partner investors
	•	Operating expense deduction against AWS/Anthropic taxable income
Net Anthropic contribution after grants, credits, and tax treatment: $8–20M over 3–5 years — a rounding error on any quarterly CapEx line, against a $800B valuation and $30B ARR.
8. The ask
A pilot partnership at Rainier Phase 1, structured as follows: Anthropic provides (a) a public commitment to tenant-level circular-economy reporting, (b) co-financing of up to $20M toward the regenerative jacket, and (c) introduction to AWS sustainability leadership for heat-offtake and condensate-offtake agreements. Atlas Lattice Foundation and partner ag entities provide the operating entity, grant-stacking, and community partnerships.
The document Anthropic gets at the end of Year 1 is the first tenant-level circular-economy report in frontier AI — an artifact the company can use as the template for every subsequent site partnership, and a credible addition to its existing leadership on AI safety transparency.
