The Rainier Regenerative Jacket
A tenant-led proposal for circular infrastructure at frontier AI scale
Prepared by: Atlas Lattice Foundation  •  Version 2  •  April 2026
Janus was the Roman god of doorways, depicted with two faces: one looking at the past, one at the future. Infrastructure decisions at hyperscale are Janus decisions. The question is whether we are looking in both directions — or only the profitable one.  — opening frame

Executive summary
Project Rainier, at full 2.4 GW buildout, will be the largest single concentration of compute in human history. It currently follows an extractive template: withdrawing water from the local watershed, rejecting thermal energy as waste, and accumulating political and regulatory exposure. The archaeological record documents, at civilizational scale, what this template produces over long horizons.
This memo proposes a tenant-led pilot at Rainier Phase 1 in which Anthropic, building on its February 2026 commitment to cover grid-interconnection costs and community impacts, extends that logic to water and site circularity. The pilot is modular: reporting framework first, engineering diligence second, low-regret reuse pathways third, broader community-benefit infrastructure only after earlier modules succeed.
The financial argument is asymmetric: net Anthropic contribution is estimated in the $8–20M range over 3–5 years after grants, tax credits, and partner financing; permit-risk and community-license value at $30B ARR / $800B valuation scale is materially larger than that CapEx. The civilizational argument is longer-horizon but structurally sound: regenerative infrastructure compounds; extractive infrastructure depreciates.
1. The archaeological frame
Between roughly 14,800 and 5,500 years ago, the Sahara was green. Lakes, rivers, grasslands, elephant and cattle-herding cultures. Multiple independent lines of evidence — pollen records, sediment cores, rock art, faunal remains — confirm this. The landscape transition that produced today's desert was primarily orbital, not anthropogenic, but the response to it was civilizational. Cultures that mastered water and nutrient cycling (Nile, Tigris-Euphrates, Indus) ran for 3,000–5,000 years at continuous sites. Cultures that extracted without replenishment ran for 300–500 years and became archaeology.
The American Dust Bowl is the same pattern on a compressed timescale: productive grassland converted to dust in roughly thirty years of extractive farming. The Ogallala Aquifer beneath those same plains is now being drawn down at ten times its recharge rate.
Hyperscale AI compute is the newest and largest version of the pattern. The test is whether labs building systems to help humanity reason across long time horizons can apply that same reasoning to their own infrastructure. That question has a right answer, and it is auditable.
2. What happens to Rainier's water
Using AWS's published 2024 global data-center water-use effectiveness of 0.15 L/kWh as an illustrative benchmark, a 525 MW Phase 1 load would imply on the order of 182 million gallons per year of water withdrawal; a fully utilized 2.4 GW buildout would imply on the order of 833 million gallons per year. Actual site withdrawals will depend on local design, climate, utilization, and operating mode, and this memo treats these figures as illustrative derived estimates, not disclosed site metrics.
In evaporative cooling architectures, a large share of withdrawn water typically exits as evaporative loss through the cooling tower stack, with the remainder concentrated into blowdown and related waste streams. Evaporative loss leaves the local watershed as atmospheric moisture and does not return locally in meaningful volume. Blowdown, depending on treatment, either exits the watershed or returns in degraded form. Site-specific fractions should be confirmed during engineering diligence.
This memo distinguishes two separate water streams:
	•	Cooling tower evaporation: the intentional heat-rejection mechanism of the cooling system. Not directly recoverable at scale.
	•	HVAC condensate: water condensed from air-handling and dehumidification systems serving facility spaces. Recoverable after polishing, volume is site- and climate-dependent.
New Carlisle, Indiana sits in the Great Lakes Compact region with strict interbasin diversion rules, in an agricultural belt that depends on aquifer integrity, adjacent to communities already watching xAI's indefinitely paused Memphis water-recycling project. The political exposure of a large-scale hyperscale water footprint in this region is not hypothetical.
3. The consistency gap
In February 2026, Anthropic publicly committed to covering 100% of grid upgrades required to interconnect its data centers, investing in curtailment systems, and sharing AI's benefits with local communities. The commitment was explicit and unusual among frontier labs.
At the same time, Anthropic's tenant share of Project Rainier will participate in one of the largest open-loop water extractions in the American Midwest, with no publicly disclosed tenant-level circular-economy metrics. This is a consistency gap rather than a values gap. Anthropic does not operate the facilities and therefore does not naturally own the environmental reporting boundary.
The first frontier lab to negotiate tenant-level circular-economy reporting with its cloud partners — and to co-finance the site-level infrastructure required to make those metrics meaningful — establishes the template every subsequent lab will be measured against. That is a governance primitive, not a sustainability add-on. The durable reputational asset is the template, not the greenhouse.

Why modularization matters
The strongest critique of a bundled regenerative proposal is that unfamiliar components (agriculture, community benefit entities, multi-party contracting) drag down familiar ones (reporting, engineering diligence). Modularization lets the reporting framework advance on its own timeline while the physical infrastructure components are authorized contingent on success of earlier phases.

6. The compounding case
Direct annual savings from the regenerative jacket are modest — under $1M/year in avoided water cost. The full value emerges on longer horizons through three mechanisms: risk mitigation, political durability, and template-setting.

Risk mitigation scales with company scale. At $800B valuation, a permit delay at a single major site — whether driven by community opposition, water-rights litigation, or regulatory friction — has materially larger opportunity cost than at $350B. Political durability compounds because communities that benefit from local infrastructure are more durable allies than communities that host liabilities. Template-setting compounds because the pattern established at Rainier Phase 1 becomes the default reference for subsequent hyperscale AI sites over the next decade. Of these three, the template effect is the largest expected-value driver and the one most aligned with Anthropic's broader mission posture.
7. Financing structure
Gross Phase 1 CapEx is estimated in the $33–58M range for full-scope deployment of Modules A through D. The Community Benefit Infrastructure entity structure is designed to access:
	•	USDA Rural Energy for America Program (REAP) grants: typically 25–50% of eligible rural ag infrastructure.
	•	Inflation Reduction Act clean-energy investment tax credit: 30% of eligible equipment.
	•	Indiana state agricultural innovation grants: variable, historically 10–20%.
	•	Opportunity Zone designation (site-dependent): capital gains deferral and step-up for partner investors.
	•	Operating expense deduction against AWS and Anthropic taxable income where applicable.
After grants, credits, and tax treatment routed through a separate operating entity, estimated net Anthropic contribution is in the $8–20M range over 3–5 years. This figure is non-material against current Anthropic financial scale and should be validated by a tax attorney during diligence.
8. The ask
A four-step decision-ready pathway, each step contingent on the prior:
	•	Authorize a joint feasibility study with AWS and site operator on tenant-level circular-economy reporting at Rainier Phase 1 (Module A). Estimated cost: under $500K, 60–90 days.
	•	Commit publicly, at Anthropic's discretion and only after AWS disclosure consent, to tenant-level circular-economy reporting for Rainier Phase 1 beginning in Year 2.
	•	Reserve up to $20M contingent on Module B engineering diligence, Module C heat-offtake feasibility, and matching grants/credits. Release of reserved funds is gated on each module's independent diligence output.
	•	Publish Year 1 pilot metrics as an artifact Anthropic can reference as precedent for subsequent site partnerships.
This pathway lets Anthropic say yes to Step 1 without approving Steps 2–4. It creates a real decision, not a bundled commitment. Module A alone, if it produces a workable tenant-level reporting framework, is already a meaningful industry-first artifact.

Every aquifer that is now depleted was considered abundant when extraction started.  — the historical record, summarized
Civilizations that built regenerative water and soil infrastructure ran for 3,000–5,000 years at continuous sites. Civilizations that didn't ran for 300–500. The difference is not ideology — it is arithmetic applied across a time horizon long enough to see it. Frontier AI labs that have accepted responsibility for grid impacts should extend that logic to the watersheds their sites depend on. The arithmetic is the same.

B. Water balance (to be refined during Module B)
The water balance at Rainier Phase 1 has four distinguishable streams that require separate engineering analysis:
	•	Cooling tower evaporation: atmospheric rejection of thermal energy via water-phase change. Not directly recoverable at meaningful scale. Represents the majority of consumptive water use.
	•	Cooling tower blowdown: concentrated mineral and chemical waste from circulating loop. Potentially treatable and reusable after advanced polishing; capture ratio site-dependent.
	•	HVAC condensate: water condensed on air-handling coils serving facility comfort cooling and dehumidification. Distilled-grade quality, requires biological polishing, fully recoverable. Annual volume is a function of site load, ambient conditions, and HVAC system design.
	•	Optional indirect evaporative recovery: advanced cooling architectures may create intermediate water streams recoverable via specialized equipment. Applicability is site-design-dependent.
Module B should produce a Rainier-specific psychrometric and load analysis quantifying each of these streams under actual operating and seasonal conditions. Only after that analysis can the recoverable volume be stated with precision.
C. Thermal balance (to be refined during Module C)
Of the 525 MW IT load at Phase 1, essentially all energy becomes heat. However, the usability of that heat for external purposes depends on temperature quality, which varies by cooling architecture:
	•	Direct-to-chip liquid cooling typically produces 30–60°C return water. Usable for greenhouse heating, district heat loops, low-temperature industrial processes.
	•	Air-side cooling produces lower-grade heat harder to valorize externally.
	•	Cooling tower plume is high-humidity, low-enthalpy, and not practically recoverable for external use.
The 50 MW thermal offtake figure used in early modeling is a scoping estimate; actual deliverable heat at usable temperature for external partners will depend on AWS's cooling architecture choices at Rainier and the temperature match required by the offtake partner. Module C should produce a heat quality assessment and temperature-matched offtake design.
D. Grant and credit eligibility notes
The following programs are potentially applicable; formal eligibility determinations require legal and tax counsel and site-specific application packages:
	•	USDA REAP: historically eligible for renewable-energy and energy-efficiency improvements in rural areas. Greenhouse operations powered by recovered waste heat may qualify under energy-efficiency provisions.
	•	IRA Section 48 ITC: 30% investment tax credit for qualifying clean-energy property. Heat recovery equipment and certain efficiency components are potentially eligible.
	•	Indiana agricultural innovation grants: state-level programs vary by administration and budget cycle.
	•	Opportunity Zone status for New Carlisle: requires verification. Zone status affects capital-gains treatment for equity investors in the CBI entity.
	•	Section 179D (energy-efficient commercial buildings) may apply to specific infrastructure components.
E. Ownership and contracting structure
The Community Benefit Infrastructure (CBI) entity is proposed as a separate legal vehicle — likely a B-corp or cooperative — that owns and operates the water and heat recovery infrastructure. Ownership split and governance model are diligence items. Broad structure:
	•	CBI entity owns the physical regenerative-jacket infrastructure on land adjacent to Rainier.
	•	AWS provides heat-offtake and condensate-offtake agreements (no capital contribution required).
	•	Anthropic provides co-financing (up to $20M reserved) and tenant-level reporting commitment.
	•	Agricultural operator partner (named during Module D) operates the greenhouse under long-term lease or operating agreement.
	•	Community benefit commitments codified in CBI bylaws; local jobs, food security metrics, and annual public reporting required.
F. Sensitivity ranges
Key parameters and their realistic ranges:
	•	Recoverable HVAC condensate: highly climate-dependent. Indiana humid summers favor high recovery; winters favor near-zero. Annual volume range is wide pending Module B.
	•	Usable waste heat: depends on cooling architecture. If AWS deploys primarily direct-to-chip liquid cooling, recoverable thermal energy at greenhouse-usable temperatures could be 30–60% of rejected heat. If primarily air-side cooling, substantially less.
	•	Net CapEx after grants: wide range ($8–20M Anthropic) reflects uncertainty in program eligibility, matching grant availability, and CBI structure.
	•	Permit-risk value: not directly quantifiable. Referenced conceptually, not as a number.
	•	Template-effect value: not directly quantifiable, but scales with count of subsequent hyperscale AI sites that adopt the pattern.
G. Year 1 KPIs (Module A outputs)
	•	Tenant-level reporting framework document, negotiated with AWS and reviewed by external sustainability counsel.
	•	Site-specific water-balance model for Rainier Phase 1.
